W. W. Jacobs

1863 – 1943

❦

The Biography

William Wymark Jacobs was born on the 8th of September, 1863, in Wapping, London. As the son of a wharf manager, his early life was deeply intertwined with the sights, sounds, and eccentric characters of the bustling London docks. This maritime environment heavily influenced his later literary career, providing a rich backdrop for his signature seafaring tales.

Despite his intimate knowledge of the docks, Jacobs did not choose a life at sea. Instead, he entered the Civil Service, working as a clerk in the Post Office Savings Bank from 1883 until 1899. It was during these years of clerical routine that he began submitting short stories to various periodicals, eventually finding a welcoming audience in popular magazines like The Idler and Today.

❦

His Life & Character

In 1900, Jacobs married Agnes Eleanor Williams, a prominent suffragette whose fiery political activism contrasted sharply with his own quiet, conservative demeanor. The couple had five children together. By the turn of the century, Jacobs had established himself as a financially secure full-time writer, allowing him to leave his post office job behind.

"He was an uncommonly quiet man, gentle in manner, who observed the world with a dry, ironic wit that bled effortlessly into his prose."

Though best remembered today for a single macabre masterpiece, Jacobs was primarily celebrated in his lifetime as a humorist. His contemporary admirers included literary giants such as Mark Twain, Henry James, and G.K. Chesterton. He passed away on the 1st of September, 1943, in Hornsey Lane, London, leaving behind a vast body of work that captured the distinct vernacular and humor of the late Victorian and Edwardian working class.

❦

Notable Literary Works

Jacobs was a master of the short story format, deftly shifting between lighthearted maritime comedies and chilling supernatural horror. Below is a selection of his most celebrated publications:

  • Many Cargoes — His debut collection of humorous nautical tales that established his literary reputation. 1896
  • The Skipper's Wooing — A charming, light-hearted short novel showcasing his gift for comedic dialogue. 1897
  • Light Freights — Another widely popular anthology focusing on the mishaps of sailors ashore. 1901
  • The Lady of the Barge — A seminal collection containing his most famous horror story, "The Monkey's Paw". 1902
  • Dialstone Lane — A highly praised comic novel involving eccentric plots, hidden treasure, and coastal misadventures. 1904
  • Night Watches — A later collection featuring the popular recurring character, the Night Watchman. 1914
❦

The Legacy of "The Monkey's Paw"

While his comedies brought him fame in life, his 1902 short story The Monkey's Paw secured his immortality. This classic tale of three wishes granted at a terrible price remains an archetype of supernatural fiction. It has been adapted countless times across stage, screen, and radio, serving as the ultimate cautionary tale regarding the dangers of interfering with fate.
